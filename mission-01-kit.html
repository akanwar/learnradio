Welcome to your first mission, Agent. Before radios and circuits, every secret message came down to one trick: scrambling letters so only the right person could read them. Today you build the tool, learn the codebreaker’s weapon, and crack your first real intercept.

Part A  Build the cipher wheel

Print this page. Cut out both discs along the outer circle. Place the small disc on top of the big one, line up the centers, and push a brad (split pin) through both center dots so the small disc spins.

How to use it: Pick a secret “shift” number, say 3. Turn the inner disc so its A sits under the outer D (that’s 3 letters over). Now every outer letter points to its secret version on the inner ring. To read a coded message, line the discs up the same way and go backwards.

Part B  The codebreaker’s weapon

How did Bletchley Park break codes without knowing the shift? Frequency analysis. In English, some letters show up far more than others. Count the letters in a secret message, and the most common one is probably a disguised E. That single clue can unlock the whole shift.

Stuck? Reveal the shift (for the Handler)

L → E is a shift of 7. Decode every letter by moving 7 places back (D→W, L→E, S→L …). The message begins “WELCOME AGENT AUGGIE…” and tells you your real summer mission.

Shannon’s twist: Your Caesar cipher can be broken because the same letter always hides the same way — frequency analysis sees right through it. Claude Shannon proved there is one code that can’t be broken: a one-time pad, where the shift changes for every single letter using a random key as long as the message. Try it next — and notice the catch: you and your partner both need the exact same secret key, which is the hardest part of every secret in history.
