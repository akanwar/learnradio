Operation:
Signals & Secrets

A Summer Field Manual in Radio, Codes & Information
Agent: Auggie Handler: Dad (ex–General class) Patron: C. E. Shannon Duration: ~12 weeks Method: Learn by building

Every mission this summer asks one question in a different costume: how do you turn a message into a signal, push it across distance, keep yours secret, and break someone else’s? That single thread runs from a WWII field radio, through the Bombe at Bletchley, to a radio you will build with your own hands.

Your patron is Claude Shannon — the man who proved, with mathematics, exactly what happens when we encode a message, send it, and decode it. He gave the world the bit. He showed which secret codes are truly unbreakable. And he built playful machines for the sheer joy of it — including Theseus, the maze-solving mouse waiting for you at the MIT Museum, ten minutes from home.

The Missions

00Recon
Kickoff at the MIT Museum
Objective · set the frame

Go see Theseus in person — the maze-solving mouse whose brain is relay switches, one of the first self-learning machines ever built. As an alum, tell Auggie the story the labels can’t.

Link to remember: the Bombe you saw at Bletchley and Theseus here are connected by a real wartime meeting between Turing and Shannon.

01Cipher
Crack a code by hand
Build · cipher wheel + Python cracker

Assemble the cipher wheel, break a message with letter-frequency analysis (Bletchley’s real method), then write a short Python script that cracks a Caesar cipher in milliseconds. Start here — your Codebreaker’s Kit is the companion sheet to this dossier.

Shannon layer: try a one-time pad — a random key as long as the message — and see why it is provably unbreakable, unlike the cipher you just broke.

02Signal
Make a signal you can see
Build · Morse telegraph + SDR receiver

Wire a two-station Morse buzzer so a message becomes a signal you can hear. Then plug in an RTL-SDR dongle and watch the whole radio spectrum appear on screen.

Shannon layer: the noise floor on that screen is exactly what his channel-capacity theorem is about — a clean signal carries more bits than a buried one.

03On air
Get on the air for real
Goal · pass the Technician exam

Study the new 2026–2030 Technician question pool together (free trainer at HamStudy.org), aim for the exam at a local volunteer-examiner session by August, with Dad mentoring. Build and measure an antenna to cash in the wavelength math.

04Build
Build the communicator
Build · 2× Heltec LoRa, 915 MHz

Flash Meshtastic for the instant win, then re-flash with Arduino so Auggie can change the spreading factor and watch range trade against speed. Log signal strength versus distance and graph it.

Shannon layer: that range-versus-speed tradeoff is his capacity theorem, made physical — and the graph is his theorem drawn from your own data.

05Field op
The field operation
Capstone · everything at once

Encrypt a message (a one-time pad and the AES on the LoRa channel — two eras of secrecy stacked), transmit it point-to-point across a park or between hilltops, log the signal stats, decode at the far end, and pass a fragment by Morse over the ham radios.

SSide quest
Build a Shannon contraption
For fun · one afternoon

Honor his playful side: build a “useless box” — a box whose only function is a little arm that reaches out to switch itself off. A servo, a switch, a battery. Pure Shannon.
