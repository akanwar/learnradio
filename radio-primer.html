The Radio Primer

Part 1 · Waves & Frequency
Before a single secret message can travel, something has to carry it across the empty air. That something is a radio wave — and it obeys one tidy equation you can do in your head.

What a radio wave is

A radio wave is a ripple of electric and magnetic energy rolling through space. Unlike a wave on water, it needs nothing to travel through — it crosses an empty vacuum just fine — and it always moves at the fastest speed there is, the speed of light: about 300 million metres every second. Scientists write that speed as the letter c:

c ≈ 300,000,000 m/s = 3 × 108 m/s

Every wave is described by just two numbers, and this picture names them both. The wavelength is the distance from one crest to the next. The frequency is how many crests rush past you each second.

Same width, same speed — the bottom wave just packs in more cycles
Wavelength · λ "lambda"
Distance crest-to-crest, in metres.
Frequency · f
Crests per second, in hertz (Hz). Radios use millions (MHz) and billions (GHz).

The one equation

c = f × λ speed of light = frequency × wavelength

Because the speed c is locked, frequency and wavelength sit on a see-saw: push one up and the other must drop. High frequency forces a short wave; low frequency means a long one. Rearrange the equation to find the wavelength of any band:

λ = c ÷ f for LoRa: 300,000,000 ÷ 915,000,000 ≈ 0.33 m (about 33 cm)
Every band lives somewhere on the see-saw
Try it: tune the SDR (Mission 02) to an FM station near 100 MHz and work out its wavelength — about 3 metres. Then ask: how long should its antenna be? Part 2 has the answer.

The Radio Primer

Part 2 · Antennas & Wavelength
An antenna looks like a plain bit of wire. It isn’t. Its length is a calculation — the wavelength you just worked out, turned into metal.

Why length is everything

An antenna works best when its length is a simple fraction of the wavelength — usually a quarter (λ ÷ 4) or a half (λ ÷ 2). When the length matches, the wave and the metal push each other in perfect rhythm. Engineers call this resonance, and you already know it in your body:

The swing analogyPumping a playground swing only works if you push at the right moment in each cycle. Push in rhythm and a tiny effort builds a huge arc; push at random and you go nowhere. An antenna cut to the right fraction of λ is “pushing” the radio wave at exactly the right moment. It’s timing, not force.

The Heltec antenna, to scale

For Auggie’s 915 MHz radio, λ ≈ 33 cm, so a quarter wavelength is 33 ÷ 4 ≈ 8 cm. That is almost exactly the length of the little antenna that screws onto the board. Nobody picked that size at random — it’s the number you calculated, made real.

Drawn to scale — the antenna is a small fraction of the wave it catches

The same rule, everywhere you look

This one idea explains antennas all around the house:

AM towers · ~1 MHz
λ = 300 m, so the quarter-wave antenna is a giant 75 m tower — the ones you see from the highway.
Phone Wi-Fi · 2.4 GHz
λ = 12 cm, so a 3 cm antenna hides inside the case.

And 915 MHz LoRa sits in a sweet spot: the antenna is small enough to pocket, but the frequency is low enough that the waves travel far and bend around buildings and trees better than Wi-Fi can. That balance is a big reason LoRa reaches for kilometres on a tiny battery.

The thread to remember. Wavelength is the bridge between the two halves of this primer. Frequency sets the wavelength (λ = c ÷ f), and the wavelength sets the antenna (about λ ÷ 4). Learn that chain once and you can predict the size of almost any antenna you’ll ever meet.
