Signal
02
See the Invisible
Codename: Signal · From wire to airwaves
ObjectiveTurn a message into a signal you can hear, then see invisible radio waves on a screen.

Briefing

A code hides what a message says. A signal is how it travels. First you’ll send Morse down a wire and feel a message become electricity. Then comes the best $30 of the summer: a small USB radio receiver (an RTL-SDR) that turns your laptop into a window onto the entire radio spectrum — aircraft, weather, FM, hams — all the invisible traffic in the air around your house, suddenly visible.

Field Operations

  1. Build a two-station Morse telegraph: battery, buzzer, wire, and a key made from a clothespin.
  2. Learn five Morse letters. Send your name room to room.
  3. Plug in the RTL-SDR. Install the receiver software with your Handler.
  4. Tune across the spectrum. Find an FM station, then find the quiet hiss between stations — that’s noise.
  5. On the waterfall display, point to where a strong signal stands above the noise floor.
Shannon’s twist. That gap between a signal and the noise floor has a name: signal-to-noise ratio. Shannon proved it sets a hard speed limit — the channel capacity — on how many bits a channel can carry. A clean signal carries more than a buried one. You’ll measure this for real in Mission 04.

Equipment

  • Buzzer, wire, 1.5–9V battery
  • Clothespin + thumbtacks (the key)
  • RTL-SDR USB dongle + laptop

Debrief — mission complete when

  • You sent your name in Morse
  • You found a live signal on the SDR
  • You can point to signal vs. noise
Safety: Battery circuits only — nothing that plugs into a wall. The SDR only listens; it never transmits, so it’s legal for an unlicensed agent. Transmitting comes after Mission 03.
