Brief 00 / Cambridge Station
Recon
00
First Light
Codename: Recon · The Patron’s Mouse
ObjectiveStand in front of the machine that started it all, and meet the patron of the whole summer.

Briefing

Before you build anything, you need to know whose footsteps you’re following. Claude Shannon figured out the mathematics of every message ever sent — he gave the world the bit. During WWII he met Alan Turing, the Bletchley codebreaker, and that conversation launched Shannon’s work on secret codes. He also built playful machines, including Theseus, a mechanical mouse that learns a maze and remembers the way out. Its “brain” is just telephone relay switches. The original is on display at the MIT Museum — ten minutes from home, in the institute your Handler attended.

Field Operations

  1. Travel to the MIT Museum in Kendall Square. Find Theseus.
  2. Watch how it “remembers” a path it has already solved. Ask: where is the memory actually stored?
  3. Handler’s briefing: hear the story the labels don’t tell — the bit, the Turing meeting, why a master’s thesis changed the world.
  4. Make the connection out loud: the Bombe you saw at Bletchley and this mouse are linked by one real wartime friendship.
  5. Debrief over food. Sketch how a relay works from memory.

Equipment

  • Field notebook + pencil
  • Museum admission
  • One curious agent

Debrief — mission complete when

  • You can explain what a relay does
  • You can say why Theseus “remembers”
  • You can link the Bombe to the mouse
Handler note: No gadgets yet on purpose. This mission is about awe — the feeling that the whole summer is one continuous story you’re about to join.
Brief 01 / Cambridge Station
Cipher
01
The Secret Life of Letters
Codename: Cipher · Companion sheet: Codebreaker’s Kit
ObjectiveHide a message so no one can read it — then break one that someone tried to hide from you.

Briefing

Long before radios, every secret came down to scrambling letters. Today you build the tool the spies used, learn the weapon the codebreakers used against it, and crack a real intercept. Then you meet the one code that can never be broken. Your printed Codebreaker’s Kit is the hands-on half of this mission — keep it beside you.

Field Operations

  1. Build the cipher wheel from the Kit. Send each other messages with a secret shift.
  2. Learn frequency analysis: count the letters, the most common one is probably a hidden E.
  3. Crack the first intercept in the Kit using only the frequency trick. Report the decoded order to your Handler.
  4. Write the Python Caesar-cracker together — watch a computer break in milliseconds what you just did by hand.
  5. Try a one-time pad: a random key, as long as the message, a different shift for every letter.
Shannon’s twist. Your Caesar cipher breaks because each letter always hides the same way. Shannon proved the one-time pad has perfect secrecy — truly unbreakable — but only if you and your partner share the exact same secret key. That key-sharing problem is the hardest part of every secret in history.

Equipment

  • Printed Codebreaker’s Kit
  • Scissors + a brad (split pin)
  • Laptop with Python

Debrief — mission complete when

  • You cracked the intercept by hand
  • The Python cracker runs
  • You can explain the one-time pad’s catch
Handler note: Resist giving the shift away. The struggle is the lesson — the reveal is on the Kit for emergencies only.
Brief 02 / Cambridge Station
Signal
02
Make It Visible
Codename: Signal · From wire to airwaves
ObjectiveTurn a message into a signal you can hear, then see invisible radio waves on a screen.

Briefing

A code hides what a message says. A signal is how it travels. First you’ll send Morse down a wire and feel a message become electricity. Then comes the best $30 of the summer: a small USB radio receiver (an RTL-SDR) that turns your laptop into a window onto the entire radio spectrum — aircraft, weather, FM, hams — all the invisible traffic in the air around your house, suddenly visible.

Field Operations

  1. Build a two-station Morse telegraph: battery, buzzer, wire, and a key made from a clothespin.
  2. Learn five Morse letters. Send your name room to room.
  3. Plug in the RTL-SDR. Install the receiver software with your Handler.
  4. Tune across the spectrum. Find an FM station, then find the quiet hiss between stations — that’s noise.
  5. On the waterfall display, point to where a strong signal stands above the noise floor.
Shannon’s twist. That gap between a signal and the noise floor has a name: signal-to-noise ratio. Shannon proved it sets a hard speed limit — the channel capacity — on how many bits a channel can carry. A clean signal carries more than a buried one. You’ll measure this for real in Mission 04.

Equipment

  • Buzzer, wire, 1.5–9V battery
  • Clothespin + thumbtacks (the key)
  • RTL-SDR USB dongle + laptop

Debrief — mission complete when

  • You sent your name in Morse
  • You found a live signal on the SDR
  • You can point to signal vs. noise
Safety: Battery circuits only — nothing that plugs into a wall. The SDR only listens; it never transmits, so it’s legal for an unlicensed agent. Transmitting comes after Mission 03.
Brief 03 / Cambridge Station
On Air
03
Earn Your Callsign
Codename: On Air · The licensed operator
ObjectivePass the FCC Technician exam and get legally on the air — with a real callsign of your own.

Briefing

So far you’ve only listened. To transmit on the ham bands you need a license — and you can earn one. The Technician exam is 35 multiple-choice questions drawn from a public pool, and a brand-new 2026–2030 pool takes effect July 1, so you’ll study only current material. Your Handler held a General-class license and can mentor every step, and once you pass, get on the air with you.

Field Operations

  1. Make a free account at HamStudy.org. Take the practice exam cold to see the starting line.
  2. Study a little daily — the trainer repeats the questions you miss. Pair it with the ARRL Technician manual.
  3. Build and measure a simple antenna; cash in the wavelength math (λ = speed ÷ frequency).
  4. Find a local volunteer-examiner (VE) test session. Register to sit the exam.
  5. Pass the Technician exam. Receive your callsign. Make your first supervised contact.

Equipment

  • HamStudy.org (free)
  • ARRL Technician manual, 2026–2030
  • A handheld radio (Handler’s)

Debrief — mission complete when

  • Practice scores clear 85%
  • You pass the Technician exam
  • You make a first contact on air
Rule of the air: Never transmit on the ham bands until your callsign is issued — that’s the law. Until then, supervised practice on the Handler’s license only. This is the longest mission; start it early and let it run alongside the others.
Brief 04 / Cambridge Station
Build
04
Build the Communicator
Codename: Build · LoRa, 915 MHz
ObjectiveBuild a pair of long-range radios from scratch and prove Shannon’s limit with your own data.

Briefing

Now everything converges. Two small boards — Heltec WiFi LoRa 32 V3, the US 915 MHz version — become a working point-to-point communicator that needs no cell signal or internet. You’ll get the instant win first, then open the hood and start changing the radio’s settings to watch the laws of information play out.

Field Operations

  1. Order two Heltec V3 boards — confirm 915 MHz, not 868 (wrong band = nothing works).
  2. Flash both with Meshtastic using the web flasher. Pair each to a phone. Send your first off-grid message.
  3. Re-flash with Arduino + RadioLib for the real lesson: a messenger you control.
  4. Change the spreading factor (SF7–SF12). Higher = longer range but slower. Watch the tradeoff.
  5. Range test: walk apart in steps, log signal strength (RSSI) at each distance, then graph RSSI vs. distance.
Shannon’s twist. That spreading-factor dial is his channel-capacity theorem in your hand: trade speed for the ability to claw a readable signal out of the noise at greater range. Your RSSI-vs-distance graph is Shannon’s law, drawn from data you collected yourself.

Equipment

  • 2× Heltec WiFi LoRa 32 V3, 915 MHz
  • 2× antennas + 2× LiPo batteries
  • Laptop, Arduino IDE, 2 phones

Debrief — mission complete when

  • A message crosses between the two boards
  • You have an RSSI-vs-distance graph
  • You can explain the SF tradeoff
Safety: Always attach the antenna before powering a board that transmits. Handle LiPo batteries gently — no bending, crushing, or charging unattended.
Brief 05 / Cambridge Station
Field Op
05
The Field Operation
Codename: Field Op · The capstone
ObjectiveRun one real operation that uses every skill of the summer at once.

Briefing

This is the mission you’ll remember. A secret message, encrypted two ways, transmitted across real distance on a radio you built, logged, decoded, and confirmed — with a fragment passed by Morse over the ham radios for good measure. Codes, signals, hardware, and history, all firing together. Plan it like the operation it is.

Field Operations

  1. Write the message. Encrypt it with your one-time pad (Mission 01).
  2. Send it over the LoRa link, whose channel is itself AES-encrypted — two eras of secrecy stacked.
  3. Deploy to two points — across a park, or two hilltops. Set spreading factor for the distance.
  4. Transmit. Log RSSI and SNR for the link (Mission 02 & 04).
  5. Decode at the far end. Pass one fragment back by Morse on the ham radios (Mission 03). Write the after-action report.

Equipment

  • Both LoRa boards, charged + antennas
  • One-time pad key (two copies)
  • Ham radios, notebook, two operators

Debrief — mission complete when

  • The message arrives and decodes cleanly
  • Signal stats are logged
  • The after-action report is written
Handler note: Let Auggie run it — pick the site, set the parameters, call the shots. You’re the second operator, not the commander. The after-action report is his to author.
Brief S / Cambridge Station
Side Quest
S
The Useless Box
Codename: Side Quest · In the spirit of Shannon
ObjectiveBuild something gloriously pointless, exactly the way the patron would have wanted.

Briefing

Shannon built machines purely for delight — a juggling robot, a flame-throwing trumpet, and the famous useless box: flip its switch on, and a little arm reaches out to switch itself off. That’s its entire purpose. The MIT Museum keeps his whimsical contraptions because play and genius were the same thing to him. Slot this in whenever you need a pure-fun afternoon.

Field Operations

  1. Gather a hobby servo, a toggle switch, a battery, and a small box.
  2. Wire it so flipping the switch ON drives the servo arm to flip it back OFF.
  3. Decorate the box. Give the machine a name.
  4. Flip it on. Watch it refuse. Laugh.

Equipment

  • Hobby servo + toggle switch
  • Battery + small box
  • Glue, paint, imagination

Debrief — mission complete when

  • The arm turns itself off
  • It made someone laugh
  • You felt like Shannon for a minute
Handler note: There’s no Shannon “twist” here because the whole mission is the twist — the point of genius is that it can also just be fun.
